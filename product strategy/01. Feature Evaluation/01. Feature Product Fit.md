### Feature Product Fit

In this module, we discuss the transition from Senior Product Manager (SPM) to Product Leader, focusing on expanding product strategy beyond the tactical to strategic thinking. The module emphasizes the importance of understanding four key product work areas: Feature Strategy, Growth Strategy, Product-Market Fit (PMF) Expansion, and Scaling. 

#### Key Transitions for Product Leaders:
1. **Depth in One Type of Product Work to Breadth Across Multiple Product Work Types**: Product leaders must broaden their expertise from being deep in one area (e.g., specific product features) to overseeing various types of product strategies.
2. **Gaining More Personal Scope to Creating Scope for the Organization**: The shift from personal responsibility for features to creating an organizational impact where product leaders guide teams across the product strategy landscape.
3. **Influencing Specific Product Outcomes to Influencing Broader Product Strategy**: Product leaders shift from focusing on specific feature outcomes to overseeing the overarching product strategy that influences the entire product lifecycle.

#### The Four Strategic Levers:
1. **Feature Strategy**:
   - Focuses on enhancing the existing value proposition of the product by adding incremental value or extending it to new user groups.
   - Example: Figma improving its designer annotations feature for better user cases or adding engineering collaboration features for broader audience engagement.
   - After achieving PMF, product leaders shift from managing individual features to overseeing a portfolio of features, guiding their success over time.

2. **Growth Strategy**:
   - Aims to maximize the existing value proposition, improving access, distribution, and engagement within the current market.
   - Example: Improving Figma’s onboarding process to boost user acquisition and retention.

3. **Product-Market Fit (PMF) Expansion Strategy**:
   - Focuses on expanding into new markets or adding new value propositions to the product.
   - Example: Figma launching in a new market like Asia or adding a video editing feature to broaden its appeal.

4. **Scaling**:
   - Involves strategic investments in infrastructure to support continued growth and product delivery.
   - Example: Managing technical debt or developing an experimentation system.

#### Feature Product Fit:
Feature Product Fit (FPF) occurs when features meaningfully contribute to the product’s value proposition and help it create or capture value in three ways: **acquisition**, **retention**, and **monetization**.

- **Acquisition**: A feature can help attract new users, like Robinhood offering free stocks for referrals.
- **Retention**: A feature that encourages users to keep coming back, like Netflix’s "Skip Intro" feature or HubSpot's email tracking feature that keeps users engaged.
- **Monetization**: A feature driving revenue generation, such as Spotify’s offline download feature, which is a premium feature that drives conversions to paying subscribers.

#### Evaluating Feature Performance:
To evaluate a feature's contribution to the product’s value, product leaders focus on three key metrics:
1. **Adoption**: The share of intended users who start using the feature.
2. **Retention**: The share of users who continue using the feature over time.
3. **Satisfaction**: The share of retained users who report high satisfaction with the feature.

#### Feature Strategy Framework:
Product leaders should develop a comprehensive feature strategy using a feature map template, which includes:
- **Qualitative properties**: Defining the target population, user value, and business value for each feature.
- **TARS Framework**: Used to assess feature adoption, retention, and satisfaction.
- **Feature Matrix**: A tool to view feature performance across the portfolio, guiding the overall strategy.
- **Continuous Feature Evaluation**: After PMF, product leaders must consistently evaluate, enhance, and remove features to maintain a healthy product strategy and meet evolving customer expectations.

### **I-Shaped vs. T-Shaped Skills**

#### **I-Shaped Skills**
- **Definition**: I-shaped skills refer to deep expertise in one specific area or discipline. An individual with I-shaped skills is highly specialized and focused on one particular skill or field, often becoming an expert in that narrow domain.
  
- **Key Characteristics**:
  - Deep knowledge in one area.
  - Strong technical or subject matter expertise.
  - Limited breadth in other areas.

- **Example**: A software developer with highly specialized expertise in a specific programming language or a data scientist who is an expert in one type of statistical model.

- **Pros**:
  - Mastery of a particular skill.
  - High productivity and performance in the specialized area.
  
- **Cons**:
  - Limited ability to collaborate across different domains.
  - Hard to adapt to different roles or change direction within a company.
  - Lack of broader organizational impact.

---

#### **T-Shaped Skills**
- **Definition**: T-shaped skills refer to individuals who have a broad base of general knowledge (the top of the "T") across several disciplines or areas, coupled with deep expertise (the vertical line of the "T") in one specific area. These individuals can collaborate across functions and have the flexibility to work in multiple areas while leveraging their deep expertise when necessary.

- **Key Characteristics**:
  - **Horizontal (T-top)**: Broad understanding across multiple disciplines (e.g., business, design, technology, etc.).
  - **Vertical (T-stem)**: Deep expertise in one specific domain.
  - **Adaptability**: Ability to work across teams and understand different areas, while still being able to provide expert-level input in one field.

- **Example**: A product manager who understands business strategy, user experience, data analytics, and engineering principles but has deep expertise in product design or user research.

- **Pros**:
  - Greater collaboration across different teams.
  - Flexible and adaptable in various roles.
  - Broad skillset enables problem-solving across diverse situations.
  
- **Cons**:
  - Less depth in any single domain compared to I-shaped individuals.
  - May not have the same level of technical mastery in their area as a specialized expert.

### **Which One is Better?**
- **I-Shaped Skills**: Best for roles that require deep technical expertise or specialization, where focus and mastery are necessary. For example, a software architect or a scientist specializing in a particular field.
  
- **T-Shaped Skills**: Best for roles where collaboration, adaptability, and a broad understanding of multiple areas are crucial. This is especially important for leadership roles, product managers, or team leads who need to bridge different areas of expertise and drive cross-functional collaboration.

---

### **How to Develop T-Shaped Skills**
1. **Expand Your Knowledge Base (Top of the T)**: Take courses, read books, and participate in cross-functional projects to build knowledge in areas outside of your expertise.
2. **Leverage Your Expertise (Stem of the T)**: Focus on deepening your understanding of your primary area of expertise while developing other areas.
3. **Collaborate Across Disciplines**: Engage with people from different departments and learn about their work. Participate in cross-functional teams or projects.
4. **Stay Curious and Adaptable**: Always be open to learning and expanding your skills.

T-shaped skills provide versatility, enabling individuals to contribute meaningfully across various domains while still leveraging deep expertise when needed. This makes them ideal for leadership, product management, or any role that requires a mix of specialization and cross-functional collaboration.

### **Explanation of the Mistakes in Feature Strategy**

#### **1. Evaluating Product-Level Performance, but Not Individual Feature Performance**
- **Explanation**: Evaluating product-level performance alone may overlook problems at the individual feature level. It’s possible that a feature is underperforming, but the overall product metrics mask these issues. Focusing only on the product as a whole might not provide insights into which specific features need attention or improvement.
  
- **Key Idea**: To effectively manage a product, it’s crucial to evaluate each feature individually, understanding how each one contributes to the overall product success. Relying solely on product-level metrics can obscure performance problems tied to specific features.

- **Example**:  
  If an e-commerce platform’s overall sales are high, product leaders might assume everything is working well. However, by evaluating individual features like the search functionality or the payment gateway, they might find that users are abandoning their carts due to poor payment options or a confusing search interface.

- **How-To**:
  1. **Isolate Feature Metrics**: Track and measure key performance indicators (KPIs) for each feature independently.
  2. **Segment Data**: Break down product performance by features, looking at adoption rates, user feedback, and engagement.
  3. **Perform A/B Tests**: Conduct tests on specific features to validate whether improvements drive better performance.
  4. **Drill Down into the Data**: Use feature-specific metrics to identify underperforming areas and address them promptly.

---

#### **2. Underestimating the Value Existing Features Can Create**
- **Explanation**: Many product leaders focus too much on new features and neglect the potential value existing features can bring by being enhanced. Existing features that are working well could be expanded, improved, or adapted to better serve the current user base and unlock more value.
  
- **Key Idea**: Enhancing existing features can often provide higher return on investment (ROI) than launching new features, as it strengthens the current product and user experience.

- **Example**:  
  A social media platform might launch a new feature for live video streaming, but it could improve existing features like messaging or notifications instead, which would drive higher engagement and retention, especially if users have already adopted those features.
  
- **How-To**:
  1. **Identify Key Existing Features**: Review the current features that users interact with the most.
  2. **Collect User Feedback**: Use surveys, interviews, and support data to understand how users feel about these existing features.
  3. **Enhance Features Incrementally**: Implement small changes or improvements to existing features based on feedback.
  4. **Track Performance Impact**: Measure the impact of enhancements on user retention, satisfaction, and engagement.

### **Feature vs. Product: Key Differences**

#### **1. Definition**
- **Feature**: A feature is a distinct attribute, functionality, or characteristic within a product that addresses a specific user need or problem. Features are often individual components of a larger product designed to enhance the user experience or offer a specific benefit.
  - **Example**: In an e-commerce app, the search functionality, filters, or product recommendations are all individual features.
  
- **Product**: A product is a complete offering that provides a solution to a broader user need or market. It is the full package of features, services, and value that the user engages with.
  - **Example**: An e-commerce app is the product, which may contain features like a search bar, payment options, and product reviews.

#### **2. Scope**
- **Feature**: A feature typically has a narrower scope, focusing on solving a specific aspect of the user journey or experience.
  - **Example**: A mobile app’s push notification system that informs users about new updates or offers.
  
- **Product**: A product has a wider scope, encompassing multiple features that together solve a broader problem or provide a solution to the user.
  - **Example**: The mobile app itself, which includes features such as notifications, product listings, cart, checkout, and order tracking.

#### **3. Focus**
- **Feature**: The focus is on a single, specific functionality that contributes to the overall value of the product.
  - **Example**: A video streaming platform may add a new feature to allow users to download videos for offline viewing.
  
- **Product**: The focus is on the overall solution, its usability, user experience, and achieving the product’s goals and value proposition.
  - **Example**: A video streaming platform’s product includes streaming, searching, recommendations, playback quality, and other features.

#### **4. User Interaction**
- **Feature**: A feature often interacts with users in a limited, focused way. It might be something users access infrequently or interact with only as part of their broader use of the product.
  - **Example**: The “dark mode” option in an app is a feature that users may choose to enable, but it's just one option within the app.
  
- **Product**: A product encompasses the entire user experience and the cumulative value users derive from interacting with all its features over time.
  - **Example**: The whole mobile app experience is the product, with users regularly interacting with its many features.

#### **5. Lifecycle**
- **Feature**: Features are often updated, improved, or replaced over time based on user feedback, technological advancements, or evolving business needs. A feature may come and go, or be modified to suit new requirements.
  - **Example**: A messaging feature in an app may be updated to allow sending videos or voice messages.

- **Product**: A product is continuously refined and improved over its lifecycle, with features added, adjusted, or removed as part of ongoing updates to meet user needs, fix bugs, and stay competitive in the market.
  - **Example**: The product (e.g., an e-commerce app) goes through major versions or updates that introduce new features or redesign the user experience.

#### **6. Strategy**
- **Feature**: Feature strategy is focused on specific functionalities, deciding what features to prioritize, develop, and refine to solve particular pain points or enhance user engagement.
  - **Example**: Deciding whether to build a feature that allows for voice search in an app based on user demand and market trends.

- **Product**: Product strategy focuses on the broader picture, such as product vision, market positioning, user personas, pricing, distribution, and how to create value with a full suite of features.
  - **Example**: Creating a strategy for an e-commerce platform that focuses on expanding the product offerings, targeting new markets, or improving the overall shopping experience.

### **Feature Bundles: Enhanced Product Bundle**

#### **Explanation:**
Once a product reaches Product-Market Fit (PMF), the focus shifts to enhancing the product by adjusting the feature set. Features can be:
1. **Added** – New features can be introduced to offer more value to users or expand the product’s capabilities.
2. **Changed** – Existing features may be improved or modified based on user feedback, market demands, or performance evaluations.
3. **Removed** – Features that no longer serve the product’s goals or are underperforming might be removed to streamline the product and improve the user experience.

#### **Key Idea**:
After PMF, product leaders should focus on evolving the product by refining and bundling features in a way that maximizes their value and enhances the overall product experience. The goal is not just to introduce new features, but to ensure each feature contributes meaningfully to the product’s strategic vision.

#### **Example:**
Consider a **ride-sharing app** like Uber:
- **Added**: A feature like **in-app safety checks** or **ride scheduling** might be introduced to enhance customer experience after the app achieves PMF.
- **Changed**: The **payment processing** system might be updated to include newer, faster payment methods, or adjust fee structures based on user behavior.
- **Removed**: A feature like **driver rating**, if it’s not adding value or hindering the user experience, might be removed or adjusted to a different model.

#### **Feature Bundle (Post-PMF) Example:**
- **Book a Ride**: The core functionality that allows users to book a ride.
- **GPS Tracking**: Critical for both the driver and user to track the ride.
- **Maps**: Integrated with GPS for real-time route optimization.
- **Payment Processing**: The payment gateway to process transactions securely.
- **Driver Rating**: Allows users to rate drivers after the ride.
- **Surge Pricing Multiplier**: Adds dynamic pricing based on demand.
- **Supply & Demand Algorithms**: Optimizes driver availability and reduces waiting times.
- **Emergency & Safety**: A feature that provides safety measures during rides, like SOS alerts.

---

### **How to Approach Feature Bundles After PMF:**

1. **Evaluate Current Features**: Regularly assess how each feature is performing. Identify underperforming features, and analyze why they might not be adding value.
   
2. **Prioritize Based on User Needs**: Focus on features that enhance the user experience or solve critical problems. Remove or adjust features that no longer serve the core user needs.

3. **Bundle Related Features**: Bundle features that complement each other. For instance, **GPS Tracking**, **Maps**, and **Surge Pricing Multiplier** are all closely related and enhance the user’s journey within the app.

4. **Iterate for Improvement**: Features should evolve over time to better meet user expectations, improve usability, and maintain a competitive edge in the market.

By thinking in terms of feature bundles, product leaders can manage the complexity of a product while ensuring that new and improved features contribute to a seamless and cohesive user experience post-PMF.

### **Feature Strategy Process: Achieving Feature Product Fit**

The diagram above outlines a cyclical process that product managers follow to optimize and enhance their product features:

#### **1. Develop New Features**
- **Explanation**: The first step in the process is to identify new features that will add value to the product. These could be based on user feedback, market trends, or business goals.
  - **Key Idea**: Developing new features should align with the product strategy and meet the needs of the target audience.
  
  - **Example**: A music streaming service might decide to introduce a new feature for creating and sharing playlists. The feature is developed, ensuring it integrates well with the existing app functionality.

#### **2. Achieve Feature Product Fit**
- **Explanation**: This stage focuses on achieving a good match between the features and the product's overall goals. It's about ensuring that the newly developed features meet user needs and work within the broader product ecosystem.
  - **Key Idea**: Feature Product Fit occurs when the new features enhance the product's value proposition and contribute to user satisfaction and business success.
  
  - **Example**: After introducing the playlist-sharing feature, the app monitors usage patterns to assess whether it’s being adopted widely by users and if it contributes positively to user retention.

#### **3. Evaluate & Enhance Existing Features**
- **Explanation**: Once features are launched, they need to be constantly evaluated for performance. User feedback, data analytics, and other metrics help assess how well features are meeting their objectives. Based on this evaluation, existing features may be enhanced to improve their functionality or user experience.
  - **Key Idea**: Evaluation and enhancement of features are crucial for ensuring that the product stays relevant and valuable over time.
  
  - **Example**: If the playlist-sharing feature isn't being used as expected, the product team might enhance the feature by adding more social sharing options or improving the UI for easier access.

#### **4. Inform the Build Strategy**
- **Explanation**: The insights gained from evaluating both new and existing features feed back into the overall build strategy. This helps prioritize future development efforts based on what’s working and what’s not.
  - **Key Idea**: Continuous learning from feature performance ensures that the product evolves according to user needs and market changes.

  - **Example**: If the playlist-sharing feature performs well and has high user engagement, the team might prioritize additional music discovery features that allow users to share playlists with specific communities.

### **How to Implement the Feature Strategy Cycle**:
1. **Identify User Needs**: Regularly gather user feedback through surveys, interviews, and analytics to understand pain points and opportunities.
2. **Feature Development**: Develop features that are aligned with user needs and business goals. Ensure they’re integrated seamlessly into the existing product.
3. **Measure Performance**: Track the success of features using relevant metrics (e.g., adoption rates, user retention, engagement).
4. **Iterate and Improve**: Based on performance data, enhance features to optimize their impact. Remove or replace features that aren’t providing value.
5. **Strategize Future Build**: Use insights from the evaluation phase to inform the next round of feature development and overall product strategy.

### **Summary**:
By following this cycle—developing new features, achieving feature-product fit, evaluating and enhancing existing features, and then refining the build strategy—product leaders can ensure their product remains relevant, meets user needs, and continues to drive value over time.

### **PMF Treadmill: Understanding Product-Market Fit (PMF)**

The diagram above represents the concept of **PMF Treadmill**, which visualizes the relationship between **Product Value** and **Company Performance** during the journey towards achieving Product-Market Fit (PMF).

#### **Key Elements:**
1. **Product Value (Y-Axis)**:
   - This represents the perceived value of the product, which increases as the product solves more user problems or meets user needs effectively.
  
2. **Company Performance (X-Axis)**:
   - The performance of the company in terms of growth, user adoption, revenue, etc. As the product value increases, company performance tends to improve as well.

3. **PMF Threshold (Brown Line)**:
   - This is the point where the product’s value reaches a level that resonates strongly with users. Once the product reaches or exceeds this threshold, it crosses into **Product-Market Fit (PMF)**.

4. **No PMF (Red Area)**:
   - Before reaching PMF, the product’s value is not sufficient to drive significant company performance. The product is still in the early stages, and there is a mismatch between what the product offers and what the market needs.

5. **PMF (Green Area)**:
   - This is the stage where the product has achieved product-market fit, meaning the product is providing sufficient value to users, and it is supported by good company performance. The product meets market demand, leading to growth and sustainability.

#### **How the PMF Treadmill Works:**
- **Before PMF**: The product is still evolving. Even though company performance might increase slightly, the value of the product is not enough to drive significant business growth or adoption. This is the **No PMF** phase, where continuous iteration, development, and feedback collection are critical.
  
- **Achieving PMF**: Once the product reaches the PMF threshold, it means that the product has found its core value proposition that aligns with user needs. The product crosses into the **PMF** phase, and the company sees rapid growth as a result of this alignment.

- **After PMF**: After reaching PMF, the product’s value continues to grow, and company performance accelerates as the product gains more traction, scale, and user adoption.

#### **Key Insight**:
- **PMF** is not a destination but a phase that can be sustained and expanded upon by continuously improving and iterating on the product to maintain user satisfaction and meet market needs.
- The process of achieving PMF is akin to running on a treadmill: it requires continuous effort to reach the point where the product truly fits the market, but once you do, the growth becomes exponential and self-reinforcing.

#### **Example**:
- **Uber**:
  - Before PMF: Uber was in the **No PMF** zone, with limited user adoption and product offerings that didn’t fully meet market demands (e.g., limited regions or features).
  - Achieving PMF: Uber reached PMF when the product (ride-sharing service) provided consistent, reliable, and scalable value to users, leading to rapid adoption.
  - After PMF: Uber’s performance saw significant growth, and the product's value continued to improve with features like surge pricing, maps, and payments, pushing the company into the **PMF** zone.

### **Conclusion**:
The PMF Treadmill highlights the importance of continuously iterating and improving the product to achieve sustained growth. The transition from **No PMF** to **PMF** is a critical milestone for any product, and maintaining that PMF requires ongoing effort to meet evolving market demands.

- **Monetization**: Some features directly contribute to generating revenue or other forms of value.

### **Feature Bundles**
- After PMF, the focus shifts to expanding the product’s features, which can be **added**, **changed**, or **removed** based on performance evaluations. 
- For example, features like GPS tracking, payment processing, and surge pricing multiplier can be added to a ride-sharing app post-PMF.

### **Feature Evaluation**
- It’s essential to evaluate individual feature performance, not just the overall product's performance. Misleading product-level metrics can obscure individual feature performance.
  
### **Challenges in Feature Strategy**
- **Evaluating product-level performance** without focusing on the individual features can result in missed opportunities for optimization. For example, if a product has poor overall performance, but certain features are highly successful, the overall assessment may incorrectly suggest that all features need improvement.
- **Underestimating the value of existing features**: Enhancing and improving existing features is often more cost-effective and impactful than introducing new ones.

### **Metrics for Features**
- **Adoption**: Measures the share of intended users who start using the feature.
- **Retention**: Tracks how many of those users continue to use the feature over time.
- **Satisfaction**: Evaluates how happy retained users are with the feature.

### **PMF Treadmill**
- **PMF Treadmill** shows the ongoing effort required to maintain and scale the product once PMF is achieved. Even after reaching PMF, product and feature strategies need continuous evaluation and iteration to ensure long-term success.

### **Key Distinctions in Feature Strategy**
- **Product Managers**: Focus on metrics related to individual features, like adoption and retention.
- **Product Leaders**: Focus on the overall value a product provides, ensuring that all features align with the broader product strategy and vision.

---

### **Creating Value vs. Capturing Value**

#### **Creating Value**
- **Description**: The process of delivering meaningful benefits to users by solving their problems, meeting their needs, or improving their experiences.
- **Key Question**: *How does this feature or product improve the user’s life or solve their pain points?*
- **Examples**:
  - **Spotify**: Creating value by offering personalized playlists, helping users discover music they love.
  - **Uber**: Providing convenient transportation at the tap of a button.
  - **Slack**: Enabling seamless team communication with features like shared channels.
- **Focus**: Centered on **user experience** and **satisfaction**, ensuring the product or feature genuinely meets user expectations and demands.

#### **Capturing Value**
- **Description**: The process of converting the value created for users into measurable business outcomes, such as revenue, market share, or user retention.
- **Key Question**: *How does the business benefit from the value created for users?*
- **Examples**:
  - **Spotify**: Capturing value through premium subscriptions for an ad-free listening experience.
  - **Uber**: Earning a percentage of every ride booked on the platform.
  - **Slack**: Capturing value by monetizing features like enterprise-level tools and integrations for larger organizations.
- **Focus**: Centered on **monetization**, **growth**, and **business sustainability**.

---

#### **Differences**

| **Aspect**           | **Creating Value**                                | **Capturing Value**                              |
|-----------------------|---------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| **Objective**         | Deliver benefits to users.                       | Convert benefits into business outcomes.        |
| **Focus Area**        | User experience, satisfaction, and problem-solving. | Revenue, retention, and market share.          |
| **Success Metric**    | User satisfaction, adoption, retention.           | Revenue, profitability, lifetime value (LTV).  |
| **Examples**          | Spotify's personalized playlists.                | Spotify’s premium subscriptions.               |

---

#### **Relationship**
- **Creating value** is the foundation for **capturing value**. Without solving user problems or improving their experience, it’s challenging to monetize effectively.
- **Capturing value** ensures the business remains sustainable and can continue creating value for users.

---

#### **Practical Example**
- **Netflix**:
  - **Creating Value**: Providing a vast library of on-demand, ad-free content tailored to user preferences through recommendations.
  - **Capturing Value**: Monetizing through monthly subscription fees based on different plans (basic, standard, premium).  
  Netflix’s ability to capture value is directly tied to how well it creates value for users, ensuring a continuous cycle of innovation and business growth.
