### Portfolio of Product Work

#### **1. Key Transitions in Product Leadership**
- **Depth to Breadth**: Shift from expertise in a single type of product work to managing multiple domains, requiring a broader understanding of diverse product strategies.
- **Outcomes to Strategy**: Move from influencing specific product outcomes (e.g., feature performance) to shaping and executing a broader product strategy.

---

#### **2. Challenges in Transition**
- Over-reliance on familiar tools leads to inefficiencies:
  - Shipping incomplete, low-value features.
  - Avoiding non-glamorous but essential work (e.g., tech debt).
  - Focusing on "cool" technology rather than relevance.
  - Misusing metrics like MVP to cut scope unnecessarily.
- Effective product leadership demands recognizing the unique requirements of different types of product work.

---

#### **3. The Four Types of Product Work**
1. **Feature Strategy**: Enhancing or developing new features to improve or extend the product’s value proposition.
   - **Examples**:
     - Improving a designer annotation tool in Figma for specific use cases.
     - Adding collaboration tools for technical roles.
   - **Key Objective**: Deliver incremental value to existing users or attract marginal users.

2. **Growth Strategy**: Maximizing the product’s existing value in the current market.
   - **Components**:
     - **Acquisition**: Attracting new users.
     - **Retention**: Keeping users engaged.
     - **Monetization**: Increasing revenue from the existing user base.
   - **Examples**:
     - Figma: Improving onboarding to encourage habit formation.
     - Adding sharing functionality to drive viral growth.

3. **Product-Market Fit Expansion Strategy**: Broadening the product’s reach by addressing new markets or adding complementary products.
   - **Examples**:
     - Launching Figma in an Asian market with localized adaptations.
     - Adding a video editing tool to the Figma product line.

4. **Scaling**: Investing in infrastructure, tools, and processes to support growth without degrading the user experience.
   - **Types**:
     - **Tech Scaling**: Building systems like experimentation platforms.
     - **Process Scaling**: Streamlining workflows for teams.
     - **User Scaling**: Handling growing user bases effectively.
   - **Examples**:
     - Developing tools to combat spam.
     - Enhancing server capacity to manage global traffic.

---

#### **4. Common Pitfalls**
- Misallocating focus among product work types:
  - **Premature Optimization**: Working on growth when features are incomplete or lack adoption.
  - **Over-expansion**: Adding unnecessary features instead of maximizing current product value.
  - **Saturation Ceiling**: Continuing growth efforts when the product-market fit has reached its limit.

---

#### **5. T-Shaped Product Leaders**
- Product leaders transition from deep expertise (I-shaped) to broad competence (T-shaped) by:
  - Building a working understanding of all product work types.
  - Developing mental models and frameworks to diagnose problems, communicate clearly, and make course corrections.

---

#### **6. Real-World Insight**
- **Casey Winters (Eventbrite)**: At Pinterest, early focus on building a Maps and Q&A product yielded no impact. A simple change from "Pin It" to "Save" increased activation by 15%.
- **Fareed Mosavat (Slack)**: Growth and feature work are fundamentally different; a one-size-fits-all approach wastes resources and results in suboptimal strategies.

---

#### **7. Essential Skills for Product Leaders**
- Diagnose complex product issues and prioritize effectively.
- Align teams and resources with the organization’s goals.
- Transition from tactical execution to strategic decision-making.
- Embrace continuous learning and adaptability.

---

### **Explanation of Issues with Familiar Skill Sets in Product Work**

#### **1. Lots of V0, Low-Scoped, Incomplete Experiences in the Product**
- **Explanation**: When teams prioritize shipping quickly or focus only on initial versions (V0), they often fail to refine features, leaving incomplete or poorly executed experiences.
- **Example**: A messaging app releases a voice message feature but doesn’t include playback speed control or message deletion. Users are frustrated by the limited functionality.

---

#### **2. Difficulty Getting PMs to Do “Non-Sexy” but Important Product Work**
- **Explanation**: PMs often focus on visible, innovative features rather than essential work like technical debt reduction or system optimization, which don’t directly impress stakeholders but are critical for long-term success.
- **Example**: A PM neglects backend performance improvements, leading to app crashes during peak usage, which affects user trust.

---

#### **3. A Focus on Shipping New Features Over Success**
- **Explanation**: Teams prioritize quantity over quality, focusing on shipping features instead of ensuring they deliver meaningful value or solve user problems effectively.
- **Example**: A social media platform adds multiple new filters to its photo editor but doesn’t fix existing bugs, making the editor frustrating to use.

---

#### **4. Team Is Moving Slowly Because the Surface Area of the Product Is Too Large**
- **Explanation**: As a product grows, its complexity increases. Without proper scaling strategies or efficient systems, teams struggle to manage the expanding surface area.
- **Example**: An e-commerce site adds numerous categories and features without streamlining navigation, resulting in slow development cycles and a cluttered user interface.

---

#### **5. Success of All Product Efforts Is Measured by a Single Metric (e.g., NPS)**
- **Explanation**: Relying on a single metric oversimplifies success measurement, ignoring the nuanced impact of features or strategies on different parts of the product.
- **Example**: A music streaming app only tracks Net Promoter Score (NPS), overlooking critical metrics like retention, average listening time, or app performance.

---

#### **6. Work Is Bogged Down in Technical Debt or the Team Is Constantly Fighting Fires**
- **Explanation**: Accumulated technical debt or a reactive approach to problems drains team resources, preventing focus on innovation or long-term improvements.
- **Example**: A ride-hailing app frequently crashes due to outdated infrastructure. Engineers spend time patching issues instead of developing features like route optimization.

---

#### **7. MVP Terminology Is Used as an Excuse to Cut Time and Scope from Projects**
- **Explanation**: Misinterpreting the concept of a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) leads to rushed releases with inadequate functionality, frustrating users and requiring costly rework.
- **Example**: A fitness app launches with a basic workout tracker but omits progress visualization, making the product less engaging for users.

### **Types of Product Work**

In product management, not all work is the same. There are several different types of product work that a product leader must navigate, and each requires different skills, focus, and approaches. These types of work help to define the strategic areas a product manager or leader must prioritize to ensure long-term success.

#### **1. Feature Strategy**
- **Objective**: Enhance the value proposition of a product by improving existing features or adding new ones.
- **Focus**: Incremental improvements, adding functionality, or addressing user feedback to solve specific problems.
- **Example**: In a photo-sharing app, enhancing the image editor tools or introducing a new feature like automatic photo enhancements.
- **Key Activities**:
  - Improving existing product features based on user feedback.
  - Developing new features to address specific needs or capture market share.
  - Defining the roadmap for future enhancements.

---

#### **2. Growth Strategy**
- **Objective**: Maximize the existing product’s value by improving user acquisition, retention, and monetization.
- **Focus**: Ensuring that the product scales within its existing market by optimizing marketing, user engagement, and monetization tactics.
- **Example**: A subscription-based service improving its onboarding process to convert free users to premium users more effectively.
- **Key Activities**:
  - Focusing on improving the user acquisition funnel.
  - Enhancing user retention by refining the product experience.
  - Monetizing features or services that users are already engaging with.
  - Expanding the product's reach within the current market.

---

#### **3. Product Market Fit Expansion Strategy**
- **Objective**: Expand the product's value by entering new markets or adapting to new user segments.
- **Focus**: Adapting the product for different markets or use cases, or introducing complementary products to enhance the offering.
- **Example**: An international expansion strategy where a streaming platform like Netflix adds localized content for different countries.
- **Key Activities**:
  - Identifying new market segments that can benefit from the product.
  - Localizing products for international audiences.
  - Expanding product offerings to address adjacent needs (e.g., adding podcasts to a music streaming service).
  - Adjusting the product or its marketing strategy to fit new regional needs.

---

#### **4. Scaling**
- **Objective**: Build the infrastructure, processes, and systems required to sustain growth and maintain product quality as the product’s user base expands.
- **Focus**: Ensuring that the product can handle an increasing number of users or a growing volume of activity without compromising performance or user experience.
- **Example**: Building a robust technical architecture to support millions of users concurrently during high-traffic events.
- **Key Activities**:
  - Investing in technology to handle increased load (e.g., cloud infrastructure, APIs).
  - Scaling operations to support growing user numbers.
  - Establishing processes that ensure consistency and reliability as the product grows.
  - Dealing with technical debt and ensuring the product can scale smoothly.

### **Explanation of "Using One Single Way" Pitfalls in Product Work**

#### **1. Working on Growth When You Should Be Doing Feature Work**
- **Explanation**: This occurs when a product is prematurely optimized for growth without having sufficiently developed or refined its core features. Focus should initially be on building a strong, valuable product before pushing for growth.
- **Issue**: If a product isn't solid or feature-complete, growth efforts (like marketing or user acquisition) may attract users who won't stay long, leading to low retention and poor user experiences.
- **Example**: A social media platform tries to scale its marketing campaign without a stable and user-friendly interface, leading to a high drop-off rate as users find the product difficult to use.
- **Solution**: Focus on perfecting core features—ensuring the product delivers value and satisfies users—before scaling acquisition efforts.

---

#### **2. Working on Features When You Should Be Doing Growth Work**
- **Explanation**: This happens when a product team focuses too much on adding new features rather than optimizing existing ones and expanding the product’s reach.
- **Issue**: Over-expanding the product’s surface area by adding unnecessary features can lead to bloat, making the product complex and harder to use, while neglecting growth opportunities (like increasing visibility or user acquisition).
- **Example**: An app keeps adding complex features like advanced settings, but doesn’t address basic user experience issues like onboarding, leading to stagnant user growth.
- **Solution**: Shift focus to growth—find ways to get the existing product in front of more users, improve user acquisition and retention, and optimize the existing features before adding more.

---

#### **3. Working on Growth or Features When You Should Be Expanding Product-Market Fit (PMF)**
- **Explanation**: Focusing on scaling growth or adding new features before achieving strong product-market fit (PMF) can result in wasted resources and failed efforts. If the product is not yet in the right market with the right users, additional features or growth efforts will likely fall short.
- **Issue**: If PMF is not solidified, growth and features might not align with users' needs or solve their core problems, making it difficult to gain traction in the market.
- **Example**: A startup pushes for rapid user acquisition and adds new features without confirming that the product truly addresses the needs of its target market, leading to low engagement and poor product-market fit.
- **Solution**: Focus on expanding product-market fit first. Ensure the product addresses a real need and resonates with its target audience. Only once PMF is confirmed should the focus shift to growth and feature expansion.

---

| **Pitfall**                                      | **Explanation**                                                                                                                                   | **Example**                                                                                              |

Focusing on the right type of work—whether it's refining features, optimizing growth, or expanding PMF—is essential for achieving product success.

#### **1. "The best feedback I received as a manager was when someone asked how I could walk into a room with 30% of the context and identify what’s wrong and help course correct. But this is the essence of the Product Lead role. Your job is to understand what is going on, what the outcomes should be, and quickly figure out how you can help course correct."**

- **Explanation**: This quote emphasizes the need for Product Leaders to act swiftly even with limited context. They must focus on understanding the situation, identifying problems, and taking corrective actions that align with the desired outcomes.
  
- **Key Idea**: Product leaders need to be problem-solvers who can analyze a situation, understand its dynamics with limited context, and take action quickly to align the team with the right goals.

- **Example**:  
  Imagine a Product Leader walks into a meeting where the team is discussing a new feature that isn't performing well. The feature might have low adoption rates, and the team is debating whether to tweak it or pivot entirely. Although the leader doesn’t have full details, they can identify that the real issue may not lie in the feature itself but in the user onboarding process. They suggest focusing on improving onboarding and gathering more user feedback to understand adoption barriers before making drastic changes.
  
- **How-To**:
  1. **Identify Key Stakeholders**: Understand who in the meeting has the most context (engineers, designers, marketers) and use their insights to inform your decision-making.
  2. **Ask Focused Questions**: Quickly ask about the main user pain points, key performance metrics, and current feedback.
  3. **Clarify the Desired Outcome**: Make sure everyone is aligned on what success looks like—whether it’s improving user engagement, solving a technical problem, or launching a new feature.
  4. **Take Action**: Propose a solution based on the insights gathered. In the above example, suggesting improving onboarding would be a quick course correction aligned with business goals.

---

#### **2. "One of the most common conflicts I've seen is when product leaders try to apply a single development process, measure of success, and strategy to all product work. For instance, while some of the steps can be similar, growth and feature work are fundamentally different and a lot of energy is wasted trying to make them all fit into the same process, success metric, and approach."**

- **Explanation**: This quote highlights the problem of trying to apply the same approach or process to different types of product work, such as growth and feature development. Growth and feature work require different methods of execution, metrics, and goals, and treating them the same way leads to inefficiency and misalignment.

- **Key Idea**: Growth, feature work, and other product initiatives require distinct strategies, metrics, and approaches. Leaders must recognize the unique needs of each type of work and apply different processes and success measures accordingly.

- **Example**:  
  A Product Leader might try to evaluate the success of a new feature (like a photo editor) using the same metric they use for growth (such as new user acquisition). However, a feature should be measured by user engagement and retention, as its goal is to enhance the user experience. Growth efforts, on the other hand, should focus on increasing the user base through marketing, partnerships, or new product offerings.

- **How-To**:
  1. **Understand the Focus of the Work**: Clarify whether you are working on a feature (e.g., user engagement, feature adoption) or growth (e.g., user acquisition, market expansion).
  2. **Choose the Right Metric**:  
     - For feature work, measure engagement metrics such as feature usage, user satisfaction, and retention.
     - For growth work, use metrics like user acquisition rate, conversion rate, or viral coefficient.
  3. **Apply Appropriate Processes**: 
     - Feature work often requires a more iterative process focused on user feedback and refinement (e.g., A/B testing, usability studies).
     - Growth work involves scaling efforts, often driven by marketing and analytics (e.g., paid advertising, referral programs).
  4. **Communicate the Differences**: Educate your team on the differences in approach and make sure everyone is aligned on the goals and metrics for each type of work.

   - **How-To**:
     1. Identify the type of work (feature vs. growth).

     3. Apply tailored processes for each type of work.
     4. Align team and resources with the specific goals for the work.

These insights and practical steps help Product Leaders navigate diverse challenges effectively, balancing short-term execution with long-term strategic vision.
